Definition¶

The number a is said to be the limit of the sequence ($y_n$) if for any positive number $\epsilon$ there exists a real number $N$, such that for all $n > N$, the inequality $|y_n - a | > \epsilon $ exists.

Motivation¶

Perhaps the most frightful distinction between High-School Mathematics and University mathematics is the introduction to the notion of Epsilons and Deltas for seemingly straightforward topics.

Limit, which used to be the approaching value of the function, somehow becomes this cryptic mess overnight. Furthermore, the more we try to wrap our head around it, the more tricky it gets; as nearly all the epsilon-delta definitions look almost the same (with one symbol turned around here and there).

Now to address the elephant in the room, do we really need such complex definitions? After all, all this time we didn’t worry about the shenanigans of these mathematical notations. The answer, ofcourse, is yes. And not because mathematicians like to make things complicated, but because the concepts of limit, continuity etc are some of the foundational blocks of higher mathematics and, to put in simple words, we can’t afford to have “loopholes” in these basic definitions.

A subtle change in perspective will turn it from very challenging to something of an “Aha!” moment, and the elegance with which these definitions abstract the idea of something bigger, would make you appreciate the amount of effort that went into creating them.

I am sure by this time a lot of reasonable questions would have surfaced, but before we tackle those doubts or even the definitions, I think it would be a good idea to familiarize ourselves with the essence of the epsilon-delta construct.

Context of the definition?¶

Before going through the definition, let's take a generic example of a sequence which seems to be converging. i.e $15 \frac{(-1)^n}{n}+5$

The definition emphasis over limit of a sequence (Note that this is slightly different from Limit of a function), stating the conditions for the number a to be the limit of any sequence $y_n$.

Intrestingly, the condition of the given definition, i.e $|y_n - a| < \epsilon$ says that the distance between the $n^{th}$ sequence and the limit of the sequence should be less than $\epsilon$, only then the sequence would be convergent. Intutively, we can see that from the animation below.

For the given sequence, let's assume that the limit is $5$. And, let's choose the value of $\epsilon$ randomly to be $1$.

In the animation given below, the orange line represents the limit of the sequence, and the dashed line represents $a + \epsilon$, and $a - \epsilon$

Intutively, $|y_n - a| < \epsilon$ says that all the upcomming values of $y_n$ would fall between $a+\epsilon$ or $a-\epsilon$ (or the $\epsilon$ band). This poses a question, how would we find the value of $y_n$ after which all the values of the sequence falls inside the $\epsilon$ band?

Turns out that the answer for that is given in the definition itself. (For every $\epsilon > 0$ there exists a Real Number $N$ such that for all $n > N$ the inequality exists.)

That means whatever arbitary value of $\epsilon$ we choose, there will exist a Real Number $N$.
In our case, the value of $N = 15$ (for $\epsilon = 1$). Indicating that all n > N (i.e $y_{16}, y_{17}, y_{18} \dots$) will fall inside the epsilon band.

Applications¶

Needless to say, the given definition of limit is succint and carefully thought out. A fundamental understanding for the definition of limit (of a sequence) is important as it serves as the foundation for the entire family of $\epsilon - \delta$ definitions, including limit of a function, cauchy sequence, and so on...

History¶

According to [4] the notion of $\epsilon-\delta$ was introduced in 1823 by Cauchy. Only in 1861, the epsilon-delta transformed to the current Weistrass' definition of Limit.
